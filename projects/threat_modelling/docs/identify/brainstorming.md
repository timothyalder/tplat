# Brainstorming

One of the most simple ways to identify threats is to host a brainstorming session. Brainstorming can be broken down into (1) a period of idea generation, and (2) a period of filtering where the best ideas are selected. Before brainstorming, it is often helpful to define the scope of attacks under consideration.

On its own, brainstorming is not a sufficient form of threat modelling. This is because it is (deliberately) unstructured, and does not provide a high degree of confidence that all threats have been identified - i.e., did your brainstorming session end because you found all threats, or because you became tired / the meeting ended?

## Brainstorming Variants

Beyond the atypical 'free-form' brainstorming, there are several specific methods that may be used.

### Scenario Analysis

In this variant, brainstorming begins with scenarios - e.g., Chandler's Law ("When in doubt, have a man come through the door with a gun.")

### Pre-Mortem

Consider a group of those involved in a decision gathered in a room. Ask the group to pretend it is shortly after a project deadline, and that things have gone awry. Ask the group to hypothesise why they believe things have gone awry.

With this method, the natural optimism that accompanies a project is replaced with
an explicit assumption that it has failed, giving you and other participants a
chance to express doubts.

### Movie Plotting

In this variant, attacks are deliberately intended to be as outrageous and provocative as possible. This is done to encourage the free flow of ideas.
